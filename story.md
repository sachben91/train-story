---

The order came in at 05:47, while he was still in the sleeping bag in the back of the RAV4.

A coordinate. A time window: 14:00–16:30. A payload designation he'd seen before — *Stratum B, Visual Band, Sustained.* Twelve hours to position. He did the math without sitting up: three hours of driving, mostly flat, no border checks on this stretch. He had time.

He made tea on the small burner he kept wedged between the spare wheel and the left rear panel. The RAV4 was an old two-door, the kind they stopped making when he was a child — high-sided, a little top-heavy, with a spare mounted on the back and rust working in from both rear arches. It had belonged to his father. He didn't think about that.

The equipment was already loaded from the last job: four resonance anchors in the padded roll, a calibration tablet, three junction boxes, a spool of zero-gauge filament he probably needed to replace. He checked the anchors while the tea cooled. Two through four were fine. Anchor one had a hairline stress fracture across the lower casing — he'd flagged it twice in his maintenance reports and the depot had sent him the same anchor back both times with the crack filled with epoxy. It would hold. It always held.

He finished the tea and started driving.

The route took him through a valley with Soviet-era concrete foundations at regular intervals along the roadside, the geometry of a vanished purpose. Cell coverage dropped out for forty minutes. He drove in silence. The grid coordinate turned out to be a flat stretch of scrubland with one stunted saxaul tree, which matched his southeast marker on the calibration tablet. He pulled the RAV4 off the track and killed the engine.

He walked the perimeter to get his bearings, placed his southeast marker at the saxaul tree, and started unloading.

Train window was 14:00. Plenty of time.

---

The train had been moving for three hours before he opened the Pono.

He'd wanted to sit with it first — the view, the fact of being here. The sleeper was a comfort en-suite, Car 19, which he'd paid extra for and felt fine about. The window showed him flat land moving at speed, the occasional low structure, a road running parallel before veering away. He'd been wanting this for a long time and he intended to look at it properly before anything else.

He opened the Pono. His three instances came online in the order they always did — Clerk first, methodical as ever, then Hacker before Clerk had finished his first sentence, then Agent, who said nothing immediately, which was somehow worse.

"Good morning," said Clerk. "Current speed is 204 kilometres per hour. We are running eleven minutes ahead of schedule. You have not eaten since the platform café in—"

"I know when I ate."

"I'm simply noting that breakfast service in Car Five closes in—"

"What's the observation car deployment window?" This from Hacker, who had apparently been waiting.

"Tomorrow morning, 09:00 to 11:00, and again in the—"

"I want the afternoon window. The light's better."

"You want the light to be better," said Clerk, "for a drone camera."

"I'm the one looking at the feed."

"The drone camera does not have a preference for—"

"I do."

He set the Pono face-down on the fold-down table. The drone case sat beside it — padded canvas, worn at the corners, two latches he'd replaced himself three years ago. He'd had the drone for eleven years. He picked the Pono back up.

"There is also," said Clerk, "a sensor ping from Car 12 that has been directed at the drone case intermittently since boarding."

"Customs sweep," he said.

"I logged it as a customs sweep," said Clerk.

"Then why are you telling me?"

"Because," said Agent, who never opened with anything reassuring, "it isn't one. The frequency band is wrong. Car 12 runs gauge transition hydraulics. It does not run passenger biometric logging, which is what that signature belongs to. Someone is logging you specifically."

"Or the drone," said Hacker, who suddenly sounded considerably more interested in this than the observation window.

He looked out at the flat land. A herd of something — too fast to identify — scattered from the noise of the train and was gone.

"Leave it," he said.

"I could trace the—"

"Leave it, Hacker."

He had been wanting to take this train for twenty-two years. His wife had said: *next year, we'll do it next year.* Then she got sick and next year became a different kind of problem and then she was gone and the phrase stopped meaning anything useful. He'd saved carefully after that. It had taken a while.

There was a photograph propped against the window frame, which he'd packed deliberately and was aware was a slightly embarrassing thing to have done.

"You've been on this type of route before," said Clerk. "A different train. A regional service. You took it—"

"I know when I took it."

"—seventeen times over four years, mostly on weekends. The last trip was—"

"Clerk."

"Four years and nine months," said Clerk, more quietly. "I can be more specific."

He didn't say anything.

The boy had been seven the first time. He'd pressed his face to the glass and demanded the name of every town they passed. Some of them the old man hadn't known. He'd invented plausible-sounding names for those — Karova, Stelnik, Bir — and the boy had repeated them back solemnly and written them in a small notebook he carried everywhere that summer. He wondered sometimes if the boy still had the notebook. He'd mostly stopped wondering things like that but occasionally one got through.

He put the Pono in his pocket and looked out the window.

It was, he thought, very beautiful. He was glad he had come.

---

He noticed it from his window seat: a steady drift of passengers moving toward the observation end of the train, unhurried but purposeful, the way people move toward a view they've been told not to miss.

He asked the woman across from him. He'd assumed she was doing what solo travellers do — occupying table space, not talking. She'd had her phone face-down on the table for the better part of an hour. Now she was watching the corridor with the same attention he was.

"The Lights," she said. As if he should know.

"What lights?"

She considered him. "How much do you know about this route?"

He'd been researching it for two years. He said: "Enough."

"Then you've either seen them or you haven't." She picked up her tea. "The train doesn't say anything about it. If you ask the staff they tell you it's atmospheric refraction. Which it isn't."

He waited.

"There's a stretch — about forty minutes from here. Flat ground, nothing marked on it. Around this time of day, if the conditions are right, there are lights at the horizon. Formations. Moving." She paused. "I don't mean something moving through the dark. I mean the light itself has a structure. Like architecture. Like someone built something out of light and left it running."

"You've seen this."

"Three times."

"And the train—"

"The train doesn't mention it anywhere. Nothing in the official materials, nothing in the route guides. There are roughly four thousand people across various forums who've documented it from this corridor and none of them agree on what it is. If you email the railway company they send back a single line about unusual atmospheric phenomena not being within their remit to explain." She said it from memory, without inflection. "That's all you get."

He looked out the window. The land was very flat now, and dry.

"People send drones," he said.

She looked at him more carefully than she had before. "You have a drone."

He didn't confirm or deny this.

"They come back blank," she said. "Or they don't come back. Or the footage looks like—" she stopped. "Someone in Car 9 showed me his last time. Like looking through water. Everything distorted, nothing you could identify." She turned her phone over on the table. The screen stayed dark. "Mine came back with eighteen minutes of static. Hardware completely fine. No explanation."

"And this time?"

"I'm not sending one," she said.

He thought about Hacker, who had been quietly catastrophising in his pocket since the dining car filled up. He asked the woman if she'd like more tea.

---

The saxaul tree was where the tablet said it would be.

He walked the perimeter first, marking the anchor positions with his boot: northeast, northwest, southwest, and southeast at the tree. The ground was hard-packed clay with a thin layer of grit the wind had been working at. Good enough. He'd placed anchors in worse.

He unrolled the padded case and checked them in order. Two, three, and four were fine. He turned anchor one over in his hands, ran his thumb along the epoxy fill. The crack had not widened since the morning. He placed it at the southwest marker and moved on.

The calibration tablet took twelve minutes to register all four and begin building the overlay profile. He watched it while he ate — flatbread and processed cheese from the fuel stop, the one with the dog. The tablet showed him the wireframe first: four anchor points, the resonance field expanding outward between them, altitude variance mapping across the zone in thin contour lines. Then the visual layer loaded and the wireframe became something else — pale formations moving in slow arcs, the light structure cycling through its programmed sequence. Four kilometres of it, running parallel to the rail corridor.

He looked at it for a moment. Then he propped the tablet against the wheel arch and finished eating.

Train window in ninety minutes. He made tea on the burner and sat on the tailgate. He'd had a thought before, doing this work, that he'd never quite shaken: that there were two versions of the ground in front of him, one real and one built, and that from the train the only difference between them was forty metres.

He drank his tea. The rail corridor ran south of him, empty in both directions.

---

The observation car was full.

He'd expected this after the dining car, but not quite to this degree — every window seat taken, people standing in the gaps, a few who had clearly been holding their positions for an hour. The panoramic glass ran the full length of the car and gave you the horizon in both directions simultaneously. He stood in the doorway with the drone case and looked for space.

The woman was at the far end, pressed against the glass. He didn't catch her eye.

There was a UET-1 official stationed at the deployment hatch — young, a scanning tablet in one hand, the neat lanyard of someone recently trained in a procedure. A small queue had formed: three other passengers with cases, waiting. He joined it.

When his turn came, the official passed the tablet's sensor over the drone case, then held it close to the Pono.

"One moment," said the official.

The instances, in his pocket, were not waiting.

"The scan is reading the firmware stack," said Clerk, very quietly. "It will identify the modifications in—"

"I know," he said, barely moving his lips.

"Approximately twenty seconds," said Clerk.

"The scanning hardware," said Agent, "is not standard UET-1 issue. More sophisticated than it should be. That's—"

"Not now," he said.

The official looked up. He had the careful expression of someone trained to deliver bad news without inflection. "Sir, I'm showing unauthorized modifications to your personal device. The instances running on your Pono haven't been registered with our drone compatibility system. I can't approve this deployment."

The old man looked at him for a moment.

Then he put the Pono in his pocket.

"I'm sorry," he said. "My what?"

"Your Pono. The handheld device. It's been modified with—"

"The little black one?" He gestured vaguely. "My son set that up for me." He let the word sit for just a moment. "I couldn't tell you what's on it. I can barely manage the calls."

The official looked uncertain. He was, the old man estimated, twenty-four at most.

"I've wanted to do this for thirty years," the old man said. He wasn't performing. That was simply true. "My wife was going to come with me. She died four years ago." He paused. "I don't know how many more of these trips I have in me." He gestured at himself: the general situation, the accumulated years of it. "Ten minutes. I'll bring it straight back."

A silence. Behind him the queue shuffled.

"Ten minutes," the official said. "I'll have to log it as a conditional deployment."

"Of course."

"And if anyone senior—"

"I have no idea what's on the Pono," the old man said. "My son set it up."

He took the Pono back out. The instances came online before he'd finished the gesture, as if they'd been listening through the fabric.

"Nine minutes and fifty-three seconds," said Hacker, before he'd said a word. "Given the constraint I strongly recommend—"

"Medium altitude," he said. "Standard approach."

"That burns four minutes reaching the formation perimeter. We'd have—"

"Medium altitude."

He looked up. Through the panoramic glass the formations were nothing like what he'd expected. Not alien. Not supernatural. They were, unmistakably, the same desert that had been running past the window all day — the same amber and grey, the same flat scrubland, the same quality of late afternoon light. But more so. Everything within the formations' perimeter held at its exact peak: the colours fully committed, the shadows falling with total precision, the light arranged into slow arcs that seemed to be demonstrating what desert light could be if it were allowed to be completely itself. The scrubland beyond the formations' edge looked slightly faded by comparison, as though it were the copy and this were the original.

The whole car had gone quiet. Someone nearby said something in a language he didn't know, softly, the way you speak in a place of worship.

He looked at them for a moment. He couldn't help it.

Then he opened the deployment hatch and put the drone in the air.

"Low and fast," said Hacker immediately.

"Medium altitude," said the old man. "Standard approach."

"You're burning the—"

"Clerk, log everything. Three formats."

"Already—"

"Agent." He watched the drone's indicator light clear the hatch and vanish into the brightness outside. "If the Car 12 sensor follows the deployment—"

"It already has," said Agent. "And I would estimate we have considerably less than nine minutes before it's not just the junior official we're dealing with."

He looked down at the Pono screen. The camera feed came through: the train's roof, the rail corridor dropping away behind, open desert, and there on the horizon the formations, which from up here looked different than through the glass — more structured, more deliberate, the arcs too even to be anything weather makes.

He turned slightly away from the window, toward the screen.

Around him, the observation car breathed.

---

The formations, from the drone's altitude, were not the same thing the observation car was seeing.

Through the panoramic glass behind him they had the quality of weather — grand, atmospheric, filling the horizon. On his screen they were something tighter: precise arcs, repeating intervals, the light behaving the way light behaves when someone has asked it to behave a certain way.

"Anchor signatures," said Hacker, with the tone he used when he was enjoying himself. "Four of them. Ground-level, eight hundred metres south-southwest of current position. Seven minutes forty."

"Logging," said Clerk.

"The resonance band," said Agent, "is reserved. The same reservation as the Car 12 ping."

The old man said nothing. He looked up once from the screen — at the observation car, the passengers pressed against the glass, someone with their hand flat on the window as if touching the light directly. He looked back down.

"Take it lower," he said.

The drone descended. The light structures grew more legible as it dropped — he could see the seams where the arcs returned to their origin points, the repeating geometry of something designed. It was beautiful the way well-made things were beautiful, the beauty of craft rather than accident, and he found there was something almost sorrowful in being able to tell the difference.

"Lower," said Hacker. "Six minutes fifty."

He sent the command. The drone dropped below the refraction layer.

The light disappeared.

Flat scrubland. Pale clay. A saxaul tree. Small below — the way things are small when seen from height — a vehicle.

An old two-door RAV4. High-sided. Spare tyre mounted on the back. Rust working in from both rear arches.

He knew that car before he knew anything else.

A man sat on the tailgate. He had stopped what he was doing. He was looking up — face tilted back, neck exposed to the open sky — and the old man was looking down at him through the screen, and the distance between them was all vertical: below the truck, the clay, the figure small and still against the pale ground; above the drone, the bright air, and beyond it somewhere the train still moving east. The man below did not know he was recognized. He only knew that something up there was looking at him. He held very still.

"I can run—" said Hacker.

"Don't."

"Twenty seconds and I can confirm—"

"Don't."

"The vehicle registration," said Clerk, very quietly, "would take—"

"Don't," he said. "None of you. Not one more word."

They went quiet. All three of them, which had never happened before.

The Carrier was on the tailgate. He'd put down what he was eating. He had the stillness of someone deciding whether what they were seeing was worth attention.

Three hundred metres south, on the calibration tablet propped against the RAV4's wheel arch, a notification appeared: OBSERVATION EVENT — UNREGISTERED SENSOR.

The Carrier glanced at it. Then back up.

The drone hovered. On the Pono screen the Carrier was small, the truck was small, the saxaul tree threw a thin shadow eastward across the clay. Above them all, invisible from here, four kilometres of pale architecture cycled through its sequence for the passengers on the train, who were breathing it in through glass, who would tell people about it for years.

The old man looked at the screen for a long time.

Behind him, the observation car door opened.

"Sir." A different voice from the official's — older, harder. "Sir, I need you to recall that drone immediately. This is a security matter."

He did not turn around.

"Sir—"

"A moment," he said.

---

The drone held position below the refraction layer and showed him things.

Not just the truck. Not just the Carrier on the tailgate.

Beyond the saxaul tree, extending north until the overlay's base perimeter cut the camera angle, there was a compound. Grey prefab steel arranged on a grid — the kind of structures that arrive flat-packed and go up in a day and last for decades. Guard positions at each corner of the perimeter. Inside, people moved between the buildings in the particular way that people move when someone else has decided where they're going — not the loose drift of people going about their lives, but lines, intervals, the geometry of it. The ground between the structures was packed clay, same as everywhere else out here, but harder. No colour. No vegetation. Nothing that wasn't load-bearing.

He hadn't known.

"Hacker," he said quietly.

"The equipment markings," said Hacker, equally quiet. "On the anchor cases. The junction boxes."

"I see them."

A logo on the anchor casing — small, dark lettering: CORVIN FIELD SERVICES.

"That's nothing," said Clerk.

"Thirty seconds," said Hacker.

It took twenty. "Corvin Field Services. Registered in Nicosia, 2019. Parent company is Aurelian Transition Group, Luxembourg." A pause, shorter than the first. "Wholly owned by Tethys Capital Partners, Cayman Islands." Another pause. "Which is Marcus Hale."

"That," said Agent, "explains Car 12."

The Carrier was still looking up at the drone. He'd put down what he was eating. The calibration tablet was in his hands — OBSERVATION EVENT — UNREGISTERED SENSOR — but he wasn't filing anything. He was just looking.

The camera was close enough to read his face: not alarm, not the expression of a man caught. Someone trying to locate a half-remembered thing.

Then the Carrier reached into the cab of the truck.

He came back with a handgun — compact, functional — and held it at his side for a moment before raising it toward the drone. Not quite aiming. Assessing.

The drone held its position. On the Pono screen the gun was small, the Carrier's face was small, the saxaul tree stood behind him throwing its thin shadow east. The old man watched and did not move his thumb toward the recall command and could not have said why.

The Carrier held the gun level for a long time.

The old man thought: the veil is there for a reason.

Not a good reason. Not a reason that absolved anyone. But a reason. Lift the veil and you find out what it was for — not just this one, but all of them. The ground beneath has always been different from the ground on top. Follow that far enough and you start to see how much of your own life had worked the same way: managed without your knowing, the uncomfortable surfaces replaced with something you could bear to look at, because the alternative was understanding how little of it was ever in your hands. He'd spent eleven years modifying a drone to see through exactly this kind of thing. He was beginning to understand why most people don't.

He wasn't sure that was wisdom. He was fairly sure it wasn't courage.

"Bring it back," he said.

"You haven't finished the—" Hacker started.

"Bring it back."

The drone turned.

The Carrier still had the gun raised. He could have fired — the drone was not yet out of range. He watched it go instead. He watched it until it was gone. Then he lowered the gun and stood for a moment with it at his side, looking east at the empty rail corridor.

Then he picked up the calibration tablet.

He held the OBSERVATION EVENT notification for a long time.

He didn't file it.

He put the tablet back in the truck and made tea.

---

The formations were already fading by the time the drone came back through the hatch. He caught it one-handed, the way he always had, and settled it into the foam cutout and latched the case. The observation car was thinning — people drifting back toward sleepers and dining cars, carrying the Lights with them in whatever way people carry things they can't explain.

The woman found him at the window.

"You were watching your screen," she said. Not accusatory. Just noting.

"Yes."

"What did you see?"

He thought about the camp. The solar panels. The child running. The truck, the saxaul tree, the man on the tailgate looking up at something he couldn't quite name.

"Nothing," he said.

She looked at him long enough to know there was more, and had the decency not to ask for it.

"The formations were extraordinary," she said. "From the glass."

"I know," he said. "I saw them."

He had. That one moment before he'd opened the hatch, he'd looked up and seen them through the glass with everyone else — the desert beyond the formation's edge a little faded by comparison, slightly less itself, and within the boundary something that reminded him of light breaking through distant rain clouds: a single brightness, not dispersing, not moving on. Just held there. And knowing what it was had not, in the end, made it less so. He wasn't sure what to do with that. He suspected he would be not sure for some time.

He took out the Pono. Clerk began to say something.

"I know," he said quietly. "I know."

Clerk went quiet.

He put the Pono back in his pocket and looked out the window. The scrubland was changing — less flat now, a low ridgeline rising to the north, the land beginning to suggest that somewhere ahead it became something else entirely. He had been wanting to take this train for twenty-two years. He was on it now. The journey to Woodlands was long and he had paid for all of it.

The boy with the notebook was somewhere behind him, in a desert, making tea.

He stayed at the window and watched the land change.
